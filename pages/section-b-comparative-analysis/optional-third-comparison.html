One-Hot Encoding vs Word Embeddings

Prepared jointly by: Nupoor Mahajan & Hansa Gusaiwal
One-Hot Encoding represents each word as a large sparse vector with a single '1' marking its position and '0' everywhere else, treating every word as equally unrelated to every other word.
Word Embeddings represent words as dense, lower-dimensional vectors learned from large text corpora, so that semantically similar words end up close together in the vector space.

Comparison Table

DimensionOne-Hot EncodingWord Embeddings
Working MechanismFixed-position binary vector based on vocabulary index.Learned dense vector based on word co-occurrence patterns.
Computational ComplexityLow to build, but vectors are huge for large vocabularies.Higher to train, but resulting vectors are compact and reusable.
StrengthsExtremely simple; guarantees a unique representation per word.Captures semantic similarity and relationships between words.
WeaknessesNo notion of similarity; very high-dimensional and sparse.Requires large training data; harder to interpret directly.
Suitable ApplicationsSmall categorical feature sets, teaching basic vectorization.Sentiment analysis, recommendation systems, deep learning models.

When Should You Use Which?

One-hot encoding is fine for small, simple categorical problems or as a teaching example. For any serious NLP task that benefits from understanding word meaning and similarity - which is almost all modern NLP - word embeddings are the practical choice.

In plain language In one line: one-hot vectors only identify a word; embeddings also describe its meaning.
