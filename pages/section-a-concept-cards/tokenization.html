Text Preprocessing

Tokenization

Breaking text into smaller pieces called tokens - usually words or sub-words - so a machine can work with them.

preprocessingfoundationused everywhere
Prepared by: Nupoor Mahajan
1 Definition

Tokenization is the process of splitting text into smaller units called tokens. A token is usually a word, but it can also be a sub-word, a number, or a punctuation mark, depending on the tokenizer used.

2 Purpose

Computers cannot process a sentence as a single blob of characters in a meaningful way. Tokenization converts raw text into discrete, countable units that later steps - cleaning, feature extraction, embeddings - can operate on.

3 Working Principle

A tokenizer scans the text and splits it based on rules such as white-space, punctuation boundaries, or statistically learned sub-word units (as used in modern models like BERT and GPT). Word tokenizers split on spaces and punctuation, while sub-word tokenizers can break rare words into smaller known pieces.

4 Example
Worked example Sentence: "Mumbai's traffic is heavy today."
Word tokens: ["Mumbai", "'s", "traffic", "is", "heavy", "today", "."].
A sub-word tokenizer might further split an unusual word like "unbelievably" into ["un", "believ", "ably"].
5 Advantages
  • Forms the foundation for almost every other NLP step.
  • Sub-word tokenization handles rare and unseen words gracefully.
  • Makes it possible to count, index, and vectorize text.
6 Limitations
  • Simple white-space tokenizers fail for languages without clear word boundaries (e.g. some Indian scripts written without spaces between certain units).
  • Punctuation attached to words (like "don't") needs special handling.
  • Over-aggressive splitting can lose meaning carried by multi-word expressions.
7 Applications
  • Search engines indexing web pages.
  • Chatbots parsing user queries.
  • Any machine learning model that needs numeric input derived from text.
