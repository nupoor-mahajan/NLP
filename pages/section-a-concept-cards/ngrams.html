Feature Engineering

N-Grams

Grouping consecutive sequences of N words or characters together to capture some local word order.

feature engineeringcontext capture
Prepared jointly by: Nupoor Mahajan & Hansa Gusaiwal
1 Definition

An N-gram is a contiguous sequence of N items - usually words - taken from a piece of text. When N=1 it is called a unigram, N=2 a bigram, N=3 a trigram, and so on.

2 Purpose

Plain Bag of Words loses all information about word order. N-grams partially recover this by capturing short sequences of words that often carry more meaning together than individually - such as "not good" versus "good" alone.

3 Working Principle

A sliding window of size N moves across the tokenized text, and each window of N consecutive words becomes one feature. These N-gram features can then be counted, just like in Bag of Words, to build a feature vector.

4 Example
Worked example Sentence: "the food was not good".
Bigrams (N=2): ["the food", "food was", "was not", "not good"].
Notice that "not good" is now captured as a single feature.
5 Advantages
  • Captures short-range word order and context that unigrams miss.
  • Helps distinguish phrases with opposite meaning, like "not good" versus "good".
  • Easy to combine with Bag of Words or TF-IDF.
6 Limitations
  • Feature space grows very quickly as N increases, leading to sparsity.
  • Still cannot capture long-range dependencies across a whole sentence.
  • Rare N-grams may not generalise well to unseen text.
7 Applications
  • Sentiment analysis where phrase-level context matters.
  • Autocomplete and predictive text keyboards.
  • Spelling and grammar correction tools.
