Language Models

Statistical Language Models

Models that predict the probability of a word sequence using statistics such as word frequencies and N-gram counts.

language modelsprobabilityclassic NLP
Prepared jointly by: Nupoor Mahajan & Hansa Gusaiwal
1 Definition

A statistical language model estimates the probability of a sequence of words by using statistics learned from a text corpus, most commonly based on N-gram frequency counts.

2 Purpose

Predicting the next likely word - or judging whether a sentence sounds natural - is useful for tasks like autocomplete, spelling correction, and speech recognition. Statistical models provide a simple, data-driven way to do this without deep learning.

3 Working Principle

An N-gram language model calculates the probability of a word given the previous N-1 words, based on how often that sequence appeared in training data. For example, a bigram model estimates P(word | previous word) using frequency counts from the corpus.

4 Example
Worked example If the phrase "good morning" appears very often in training data compared to "good elephant", a bigram model will assign a much higher probability to "morning" following "good" than to "elephant" following "good".
5 Advantages
  • Simple, interpretable, and computationally cheap compared to neural models.
  • Works reasonably well for tasks with limited vocabulary and predictable patterns.
  • Requires far less training data and compute than deep learning approaches.
6 Limitations
  • Cannot capture long-range context beyond a few preceding words.
  • Suffers from data sparsity - many valid word sequences may never appear in training data.
  • Struggles to generalize to creative or unusual sentence structures.
7 Applications
  • Basic autocomplete and predictive text keyboards.
  • Early speech recognition systems.
  • Spelling and grammar suggestion tools.
