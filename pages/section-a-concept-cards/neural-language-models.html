Language Models

Neural Language Models

Language models built using neural networks (like RNNs and LSTMs) that learn word probabilities through training rather than raw counting.

language modelsdeep learning
Prepared jointly by: Nupoor Mahajan & Hansa Gusaiwal
1 Definition

A neural language model uses neural networks - such as Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) or Long Short-Term Memory networks (LSTMs) - to learn the probability of word sequences, rather than relying purely on frequency counts.

2 Purpose

Statistical N-gram models struggle with long sentences and data sparsity. Neural language models were developed to learn richer, more flexible patterns in language directly from data, capturing longer context.

3 Working Principle

A neural network processes a sequence of words one step at a time (or all at once, in some architectures), maintaining an internal memory of previous words. It is trained to predict the next word in a sequence, gradually learning grammar, common phrases, and some world knowledge from large text corpora.

4 Example
Worked example Given the partial sentence "The train from Mumbai to Pune was", a well-trained neural language model can predict likely next words like "late", "on time", or "crowded" based on patterns learned from many similar sentences.
5 Advantages
  • Can capture longer-range dependencies than simple N-gram models.
  • Learns more flexible, generalizable patterns instead of relying on exact word-sequence counts.
  • Forms the foundation that led to today's transformer-based models.
6 Limitations
  • Requires much more training data and computational power than statistical models.
  • RNNs and LSTMs can still struggle with very long sentences due to vanishing gradient issues.
  • Harder to interpret than simple statistical models.
7 Applications
  • Predictive text and smart-reply features in messaging apps.
  • Early neural machine translation systems.
  • Speech-to-text systems needing fluent language prediction.
