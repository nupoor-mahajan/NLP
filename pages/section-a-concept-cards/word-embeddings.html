Word Embeddings

Representing words as dense numeric vectors so that similar words end up close together in the vector space.

representationdense vectorssemantics
Prepared by: Hansa Gusaiwal
1 Definition

Word embeddings are dense, low-dimensional numeric vectors that represent words such that words with similar meanings are placed close together in the vector space.

2 Purpose

One-hot encoding treats every word as equally different from every other word. Word embeddings fix this by learning representations from large amounts of text, so that relationships like "good" being close to "great" are captured mathematically.

3 Working Principle

Embedding models are trained on large text corpora, learning to place a word's vector near the vectors of words that appear in similar contexts. The result is a dense vector (commonly 100–300 numbers) where distance and direction carry meaning.

4 Example
Worked example After training, the vectors for "good" and "great" end up close together in the vector space, while the vector for "table" sits far away.
A well-known embedding property: vector("king") − vector("man") + vector("woman") ≈ vector("queen").
5 Advantages
  • Captures semantic similarity between words, unlike one-hot encoding.
  • Dense vectors are far more memory-efficient than sparse one-hot vectors.
  • Can be reused (pre-trained) across many different NLP tasks.
6 Limitations
  • Requires large amounts of training data to produce good embeddings.
  • A single word gets only one fixed vector, even if it has multiple meanings (unlike contextual embeddings).
  • Can silently encode social biases present in the training data.
7 Applications
  • Powering recommendation systems based on text similarity.
  • Improving accuracy of sentiment analysis and text classification models.
  • Serving as the input layer for deep learning NLP models.
