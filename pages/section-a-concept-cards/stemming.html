Stemming

Chopping words down to a rough root form using simple rules, even if the result isn't a real dictionary word.

preprocessingnormalizationrule-based
Prepared by: Nupoor Mahajan
1 Definition

Stemming is a rule-based process that trims prefixes and suffixes off a word to reduce it to its "stem" - an approximate root form that may or may not be an actual dictionary word.

2 Purpose

Words like "study", "studies", "studying", and "studied" all relate to the same idea. Stemming groups such variations together so that a model treats them as one feature instead of four separate ones.

3 Working Principle

Stemming algorithms, such as the widely used Porter Stemmer, apply a series of chopping rules - for example, removing "-ing", "-ed", or "-es" endings - without checking whether the resulting word is grammatically valid.

4 Example
Worked example Words: "connect", "connected", "connection", "connections" → Stem: "connect" (in most cases).
However, a tricky case: "studies" → "studi" (not a real word, but still useful for matching).
5 Advantages
  • Very fast and computationally cheap.
  • Reduces many word forms to a single feature, shrinking vocabulary size.
  • Works well for search and information retrieval where exact grammar does not matter.
6 Limitations
  • Can produce non-words (like "studi" or "argu"), which hurts readability.
  • Over-stemming may merge unrelated words; under-stemming may fail to merge related ones.
  • Does not consider the grammatical role or meaning of the word.
7 Applications
  • Search engines matching "run", "running", and "runs" to the same query.
  • Information retrieval systems.
  • Quick preprocessing for Bag of Words models where speed matters more than grammatical accuracy.
